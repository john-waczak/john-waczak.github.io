Example: Lorentz Force Law from Lagrangian Perspective

As an example, let us consider the electric and magnetic fields. For the case of electro/magneto-dynamics, they are defined as \[\begin{aligned} \vec{\boldsymbol{E}} &= -\nabla\phi - \frac{\partial \vec{\boldsymbol{A}}}{\partial t}\\ \vec{\boldsymbol{B}} &= \nabla \times \vec{\boldsymbol{A}}\end{aligned}\] where \(\phi\) is the scalar electric potential field and \(\vec{\boldsymbol{A}}\) is the vector magnetic potential field. The force on a particle of charge \(q\) moving at velocity \(\vec{\boldsymbol{v}}\) through these fields can be described by \[U = q\phi - q\left(\vec{\boldsymbol{A}}\cdot\vec{\boldsymbol{v}}\right)\]

This leads us to the Lagrangian \[\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{2}m|\vec{\boldsymbol{v}}|^2-q\phi+q\left( A_xv_x + A_yv_y+A_zv_z \right)\]

This Lagrangian is a vector equation and therefore, the rules of Lagrangian dynamics apply to each individual component. Let’s take the x-component as an example. We have \[\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}} &= m\dot{x}+qA_x \\ \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \dot{x}} &= m\ddot{x}+\frac{d}{dt}A_x(x,y,z,t) \\ &= m\ddot{x}+ q\left( \frac{\partial A_x}{\partial t}+\frac{\partial A_x}{\partial x}\dot{x}+\frac{\partial A_x}{\partial y}\dot{y}+\frac{\partial A_x}{\partial z}\dot{z} \right) \\ \textrm{}\nonumber\\ \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial x} &= -q\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x}+q\left( \frac{\partial A_x}{\partial x}\dot{x}+\frac{\partial A_y}{\partial x}\dot{y} + \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial x}\dot{x} \right)\end{aligned}\] so that putting everything together, the Euler-Lagrange equation for \(x\) is: \[\begin{aligned} m\ddot{x} &= -q\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x}+q\left( \dot{y}\left[\frac{\partial A_y}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial A_x}{\partial y} \right]+\dot{z}\left[ \frac{\partial A_z}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial A_x}{\partial z} \right]-\frac{\partial A_x}{\partial t} \right)\\ &=q\left( -\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial A_x}{\partial t} \right)+q\left( \dot{y}B_z-\dot{z}B_y \right) \\ &= q\left( (\nabla\phi)_x-\frac{\partial A_x}{\partial t} \right)+ q\left( \vec{\boldsymbol{v}}\times\vec{\boldsymbol{B}} \right)_x\\ &= q\left[ \vec{\boldsymbol{E}}+\vec{\boldsymbol{v}}\times\vec{\boldsymbol{B}} \right]_x\end{aligned}\] which is exactly the Lorentz force law we know from E&M. This example demonstrates how the Euler-Lagrange equations do work for some velocity dependent potentials.
